Solve CS made by your friends in halligan

Solve believes

that everyone should have access to Computer Science courses. Many individuals at Tufts have worked hard to achieve this. But student demand greatly exceeds available resources. And it's getting worse. This week hundreds of Tufts students are unable to register for computer science courses due a
new policy.

Join Solve's email campaign to let Tufts’ decision-makers know how serious this problem is.

My time in Halligan Hall and Computer Science has been phenomenal. I have learned amazing skills and made even better friendships. Over the last few semesters, I have been impressed by the increase in the diversity of students in the major and in Halligan in general.

Recently, I have heard significant dismay and frustration from many students who wish to, but cannot, take more Computer Science courses. My greatest concern is that the richness and diversity of this community is being threatened by the new registration policy. Studies have shown that raising the barrier to entry for Computer Science classes disproportionately discourages enrollment of women as well as men without computational backgrounds. Based on my understanding of the new registration policy, I question how new students will be able to join our community.

Furthermore, even though I am a Computer Science major, I have struggled to get the classes I want when I want them due to limited supply and size of courses.

To me, being a Computer Scientist is not about being a strange alien who lives in a dark basement staring at 011011000111010101110110 till 3 am. It’s about being a builder, a problem-solver, a logical and creative artist who can adapt to tomorrow's opportunities and challenges today. It means so much to have the rest of the world understand, at least a bit, the work that I do and the field that I love.

With the new registration policy, this is no longer possible. Such a policy undoes the last decade of progress toward openness and inclusivity in Computer Science as a field. A progression that Tufts has been at the forefront of until last month.

Please act with alacrity to address this growing problem.

All the best,

Computer Science is a field I really want to explore more. Laptops, phones, and tablets affect my life tens to hundreds of times a day. I want to know more about them beyond their usage. I want to create with them and understand them. I always thought that an education at a liberal arts university meant that I could take classes in whatever fields captured my curiosity. I do not see how it is possible to maintain the spirit of a liberal arts education if Computer Science classes are restricted to majors.

I am currently unable to register for at least one Computer Science class. I am not a major, but I believe anyone at Tufts should be able to take any number of Computer Science classes without majoring or minoring in CS. Many majors did not declare until after they had taken a few CS classes. What if I haven't tried enough CS to know if its for me?

Halligan seems like a really fun and happening place (at least that’s what my CS major friends tell me). I feel I am now cut off from being a member of such a vibrant and diverse community. Taking CS classes would greatly help me as I head out into the professional world. Every Tufts student should have the opportunity to gain these skills and background knowledge that are integral to our information society.

I believe CS classes teach much more than just coding. They teach how to be a builder, a problem-solver, a logical and creative thinker who can adapt to tomorrow's opportunities and challenges today. With the new registration policy, I can no longer be a part of this growth and learning.

Please act with alacrity to address this growing problem.

All the best,
